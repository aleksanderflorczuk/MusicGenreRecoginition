# Assignment 9 - Results Analysis and Interpretation

## 1. Objective of the Assignment

The objective of this assignment is to synthesize the experimental results obtained in the previous stages of the music genre recognition project. Assignment 7 established a controlled baseline using classical machine learning models. Assignment 8 introduced the primary model, an RBF-kernel support vector machine trained on the same feature table and evaluated under the same split, preprocessing, and validation rules. Assignment 9 turns these empirical outputs into a structured interpretation of model behavior, limitations, and relevance to the research question.

The project addresses a ten-class music genre classification problem. Each audio track is represented by 61 numerical descriptors extracted with `librosa`: tempo, zero crossing rate, RMS energy, spectral descriptors, MFCC statistics, and chroma statistics. The target variable is `genre`, with the classes blues, classical, country, disco, hiphop, jazz, metal, pop, reggae, and rock. The original extracted dataset contains 1000 observations. After removing 13 exact duplicate rows, the final experimental dataset contains 987 observations. No missing values were present.

The weakest class is `disco`. Only 9 of 20 disco tracks are correctly classified. Its recall and precision are both 0.45, which means the model both fails to retrieve disco examples and incorrectly assigns disco to non-disco examples. This is a structural weakness rather than a single accidental error. The main disco confusion is with country: 4 disco tracks are predicted as country. Additional disco errors are distributed across hiphop, metal, pop, reggae, rock, and blues. This suggests that the current global feature representation does not capture the rhythmic and production characteristics that would make disco separable.

The second weakest class is `rock`, with 10 correct and 10 incorrect predictions. Rock is spread across several neighboring classes: blues, country, disco, metal, and reggae. This is musically plausible because rock overlaps with these genres in instrumentation, spectral energy, and timbral profile. The model does not appear to have a single systematic wrong label for rock. Instead, rock behaves as a broad central category whose global summary descriptors overlap with several other classes.

Country and reggae also show notable error patterns. Country has 7 false negatives and 9 false positives, indicating that it is both confused with other styles and used as an incorrect destination for other classes. Reggae has a better recall of 0.70 but a lower precision of 0.6364, meaning the model assigns reggae too often to examples from other classes, especially hiphop and rock. Hiphop has recall of 0.65 but high precision of 0.8667. This means that when the model predicts hiphop it is usually correct, but it misses several true hiphop examples, mainly assigning them to reggae, pop, or disco.

The confusion matrix confirms that the model handles classical, jazz, pop, blues, and metal more reliably than disco, rock, and country. Classical is the clearest class, with 19 correct predictions out of 20. This aligns with domain expectations because classical recordings often differ strongly from popular genres in instrumentation, dynamics, spectral distribution, and production style. Jazz is also relatively stable, with 17 correct predictions out of 20. Pop performs well in recall and precision, suggesting that the feature space captures enough of its production and harmonic profile to separate it from most classes.

The error analysis therefore reveals a core limitation of the representation. Global means and standard deviations of audio descriptors are informative, but they compress the temporal structure of a track. Genres such as disco, rock, country, and reggae often differ in rhythmic organization, groove, arrangement, and temporal patterns. These characteristics are only indirectly reflected in global MFCC, chroma, RMS, and spectral summaries. The model's failure modes are therefore consistent with the known limitations of the feature engineering strategy.

### C. Model Interpretability and Feature Contribution

Because the selected model is an RBF SVM, direct coefficient interpretation is not available as it would be for a linear model. For this reason, permutation importance was used as a model-agnostic interpretability method. Each feature is randomly permuted in the held-out test set, and the decrease in accuracy is measured. A larger decrease indicates that the trained pipeline relies more strongly on that feature for correct predictions.

The top permutation-importance features for the final SVM are:

|---|---:|---:|
| mfcc_9_mean | 0.0448 | 0.0130 |
| mfcc_5_std | 0.0291 | 0.0121 |
| rms_std | 0.0290 | 0.0146 |
| mfcc_3_mean | 0.0288 | 0.0083 |
| mfcc_11_mean | 0.0279 | 0.0106 |
| mfcc_3_std | 0.0263 | 0.0084 |
| mfcc_4_mean | 0.0247 | 0.0158 |

| mfcc_6_std | 0.0182 | 0.0097 |
| mfcc_8_mean | 0.0175 | 0.0113 |
| mfcc_8_std | 0.0173 | 0.0116 |
| mfcc_13_mean | 0.0170 | 0.0088 |
| tempo | 0.0162 | 0.0117 |

The ranking is consistent with domain expectations. MFCC features dominate the top positions, which is reasonable because MFCCs capture timbral characteristics. Timbre is central to distinguishing genres because instrumentation, production style, and spectral envelope differ strongly across musical styles. The presence of both MFCC means and standard deviations indicates that the model benefits not only from average timbral position but also from within-track variation.

Energy-related features are also important. `rms_std` and `rms_mean` appear among the top features. This suggests that loudness and energy dynamics contribute to genre separation. This is plausible for distinguishing energetic genres such as metal or pop from calmer or more acoustically oriented genres. However, the model still struggles with disco and rock, which indicates that energy alone is insufficient to capture rhythmic identity.

Chroma features appear lower than MFCC and RMS features, but `chroma_4_std` and `chroma_4_mean` still contribute. This suggests that harmonic or pitch-class information helps, but it is not the dominant driver under the current feature set. Tempo appears in the top fifteen but is not the most important variable. This is also plausible: tempo alone cannot reliably separate genres because many genres cover overlapping BPM ranges.

The interpretability results do not suggest obvious target leakage or an artificial shortcut. The most influential features are musically meaningful audio descriptors, especially timbral and energy features. At the same time, the importance ranking exposes a limitation. The model relies mainly on global summary descriptors. It has no direct representation of temporal groove, rhythmic pattern evolution, beat-level structure, or local arrangement changes. This helps explain why classes whose distinction is rhythmically or stylistically subtle remain difficult.

Permutation importance should be interpreted cautiously. The test set contains 198 observations, so importance estimates have non-trivial standard deviations. Correlated features can also share importance, meaning that one feature may appear less important if another correlated feature can compensate for it. Therefore, the ranking should be treated as evidence about broad feature groups rather than as a definitive ordering of individual variables.

### D. Robustness and Stability Assessment

The final model's mean cross-validation accuracy is 0.7275 with a standard deviation of 0.0130. The held-out test accuracy is 0.7121. The difference between mean CV accuracy and test accuracy is approximately 0.0154. This is not large enough to indicate a severe final-test collapse. It suggests that the selected SVM generalizes reasonably consistently from validation folds to the held-out split.

However, the train-validation gap is substantial. For the selected SVM, mean train accuracy is 0.9861 while mean validation accuracy is 0.7275, producing a gap of approximately 0.2585. This indicates that the RBF SVM has high capacity relative to the dataset size. The model can fit the training folds very strongly, but a considerable portion of that fit does not transfer to validation data. This does not invalidate the model, but it limits how aggressively the result should be interpreted.

The hyperparameter stability table provides further context:

| C=3, gamma=scale | 0.9861 | 0.7275 | 0.0130 | 0.2585 | 1 |
| C=3, gamma=0.01 | 0.9541 | 0.7263 | 0.0153 | 0.2278 | 2 |
| C=10, gamma=scale | 0.9990 | 0.7199 | 0.0047 | 0.2792 | 3 |
| C=10, gamma=0.01 | 0.9956 | 0.7186 | 0.0175 | 0.2769 | 4 |
| C=30, gamma=scale | 0.9990 | 0.7174 | 0.0085 | 0.2817 | 5 |
| C=100, gamma=scale | 0.9990 | 0.7174 | 0.0085 | 0.2817 | 5 |
| C=10, gamma=0.003 | 0.9186 | 0.7136 | 0.0121 | 0.2050 | 7 |

The two best configurations are very close: `C=3, gamma="scale"` reaches 0.7275 validation accuracy, while `C=3, gamma=0.01` reaches 0.7263. The difference is only about 0.0013. This means the result is not dependent on a single fragile hyperparameter value. There is a stable region around moderate `C` and moderate kernel width. On the other hand, larger values of `C` increase training accuracy toward nearly perfect memorization without improving validation accuracy. This confirms that simply increasing model capacity is not a reliable path to better generalization.

The learning curve strengthens this interpretation. At the smallest training size, validation accuracy is about 0.6045, while train accuracy is 1.0000. As training size increases, validation accuracy rises to 0.7275 and the generalization gap narrows from approximately 0.3955 to 0.2582. This suggests that additional data would likely improve stability and reduce overfitting. The model is not purely variance-free, but it benefits from more training examples, which is expected for a flexible non-linear classifier on a relatively small dataset.

No additional perturbation experiment was introduced at this stage because Assignment 9 should synthesize the completed experimental phase rather than redesign it. Robustness is therefore assessed through cross-validation variance, hyperparameter sensitivity, train-validation gaps, and the learning curve generated in Assignment 8.

The improvement over the best baseline is consistent in direction but modest in magnitude. The SVM improves both test accuracy and macro F1, and it has a higher cross-validation mean than the random forest baseline. However, the absolute improvement is around two percentage points. This should be described as a moderate engineering improvement, not as a decisive breakthrough. The result is reliable enough to support the selected model, but not large enough to justify broad claims about general music genre recognition beyond this dataset and feature representation.

### E. Alignment with Research Question and Hypothesis

The central research question is whether music genre can be recognized automatically from extracted audio features using machine learning methods. The project hypothesis is that a machine learning classifier trained on extracted audio features will achieve a classification accuracy of at least 70% on a benchmark music genre dataset such as the GTZAN Genre Collection. This hypothesis assumes that meaningful acoustic features contain sufficient information to distinguish between different genres.

The experimental evidence supports this hypothesis, but only with a modest margin. The dummy classifier achieves approximately 10% accuracy, which is expected for a balanced ten-class task. The strongest baseline, the default random forest, reaches 0.6919 test accuracy, which is close to the 70% threshold but does not formally exceed it. The primary RBF SVM reaches 0.7121 test accuracy, so it does exceed the required threshold. This confirms that the extracted features contain meaningful genre-discriminative information and that the final model satisfies the measurable success criterion defined by the hypothesis.

The hypothesis should therefore be treated as supported, not as conclusively proven in a broad universal sense. The results support the claim that engineered audio descriptors combined with classical machine learning can classify music genres at or above the expected 70% level on the current benchmark-style dataset. The results do not support the stronger claim that the task is solved or that global summary features are sufficient for highly reliable genre recognition.

The evidence for support consists of several converging findings:

- all learned models substantially outperform the dummy classifier;
- the strongest baseline reaches 0.6919 test accuracy and 0.6892 macro F1, which is close to but below the 70% hypothesis threshold;
- the primary SVM reaches 0.7121 test accuracy and 0.7121 macro F1, which exceeds the 70% hypothesis threshold;
- the SVM improves both accuracy and macro F1 over the best baseline;
- cross-validation and test scores are close enough to avoid evidence of severe final-test collapse;
- feature-importance results show reliance on musically meaningful descriptors, especially MFCC and energy features.

At the same time, the evidence also defines the limitations of the conclusion:

- the improvement over the best baseline is moderate, not large;
- the final SVM has a high train-validation gap;
- disco and rock remain weak classes;
- several misclassifications are structurally plausible and reflect overlap between genres;
- global feature summaries do not directly encode temporal rhythm, groove, or arrangement.

The research question is therefore empirically answered for the current dataset and feature extraction strategy: yes, automatic genre recognition is feasible from extracted audio features, and the final classifier reaches the predefined accuracy target. However, the answer is not universal. The model remains limited by the fixed feature representation and by the size of the dataset.

## 3. Academic Expectations for an Engineering Thesis

The interpretation must go beyond reporting metric values. The strongest scientific conclusion is that the current pipeline extracts useful but incomplete musical information. MFCC, RMS, chroma, spectral, and tempo descriptors are sufficient to separate some genres with relatively high reliability. Classical, jazz, pop, blues, and metal show comparatively strong performance. These genres likely have more distinctive timbral, spectral, or production profiles in the dataset.

The weaker classes reveal where the representation loses information. Disco, rock, country, and reggae are not merely random failures. Their errors are distributed across musically adjacent genres. This suggests that the model struggles when genre identity depends on temporal organization, groove, instrumentation overlap, or production conventions not fully captured by means and standard deviations. In this sense, the errors are informative: they identify the boundary of what the current engineering approach can represent.

The final model is analytically defensible because it improves over the baseline under the same protocol. It is also methodologically conservative because preprocessing is kept inside pipelines, the test set is not used for hyperparameter selection, and feature selection is rejected when it does not improve performance. These choices strengthen the credibility of the result.

The main limitation is overfitting risk. The RBF SVM reaches high training accuracy, and its train-validation gap remains substantial. The validation standard deviation is small, and the test result is close to validation performance, but the high training score shows that the model has enough capacity to memorize aspects of the training data. The final conclusion must therefore emphasize controlled improvement rather than complete generalization.

Another limitation is interpretability. Permutation importance helps identify influential feature groups, but it does not fully explain individual predictions. Because the final model is non-linear and kernel-based, its decision boundary is not directly interpretable. The feature-importance analysis should therefore support broad reasoning about feature groups, not detailed causal claims.
